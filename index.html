Why should you use mechanical keyboards?

Customizability

The ability to perfectly craft a workspace tool specifically to your liking cannot be understated. Be it switches, keycaps, custom keybinds, it all starts with a custom mechanical keyboard.

Aesthetic

There is a plethora of ways to make your mechanical keyboard your own, with keycaps matching a specific colorway to perfectly match your workspace.

Ergonomics

There are many layouts to perfectly suit your needs with a mechanical keyboard. Need a number pad for those excel sheets? Need more macro keys for editing needs? There is more than enough layouts to suit your needs.

Is there a noticable difference between regular and mechanical?

The main factor that differentiates mechanical keyboards from typical office keyboards is the action that causes a keypress. Typical office keyboards use a membrane sheet to activate keypresses, while mechanical keyboards use a mechanical switch to activate keypresses.

There are pros and cons between mechanical keyboards and membrane keyboards. Membranes require less overall maintenance as it has less moving parts, and they all use the same activation method to register a keypress, making them more preferred in large workspaces.

Mechanical keyboards tend to last longer, as they use switches that can be pressed for more than 10 million times. They also can have variable layouts, allowing more customizability for special cases where a typical keyboard won't give you that kind of freedom.

What about switches?

Linears

Linear switches are the most common switch type in mechanical keyboards. They have a smooth travel from activation to bottom out, unlike the following switch types, tactile and clicky. Grab these if you are unsure about what type of switch you want.

Tactile

In stark contrast to linear switches, these have a small bump on the way down from a keypress to indicate that the switch has been pressed. These are more popular amongst typists, as accidental keypresses are far less common.

Clickies

Clickies are essentially tactile switches that also include a click sound to indicate a keypress. These are most often the first type of switch you encounter on a mechanical keyboard. They can be very nice to type on, but your office mates, neither will your roommates appreciate the loud sounds they can make.
